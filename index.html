The story

In the beginning, the world was dark. Raven, always curious, always hungry, heard that a great chief hoarded all the light in three boxes hidden in his house deep in the forest.

Jáadee k'ínid'áan
yaa-dee k'ee-nee-daan
"The light is coming" — a phrase spoken at dawn

Raven transformed himself into a spruce needle and floated down the river until the chief's daughter swallowed him with her water. She became pregnant, and Raven was born into the house, disguised as a grandchild.

Yáahl — K'aayyángs
yahl — k'aay-yangs
"Raven — the transformer, the trickster"

The child cried and cried until the old chief relented and gave him the first box to play with — and in a flash, Raven took his true form, burst through the smoke hole, and released the stars across the sky.

Cultural quiz Meaning in context sourced from: Three Sisters recipe

"The Three Sisters are planted together because they help each other grow — corn provides the stalk, beans fix nitrogen, squash shades the soil. In Haudenosaunee understanding, this is called ___."

That's right. The Three Sisters aren't just a farming method — they're a lesson in reciprocity. Haudenosaunee oral tradition uses this relationship as a model for how communities should care for one another. This cultural teaching is embedded in the harvest stew recipe archived in adi.

What does "Aaniin" mean exactly?
adi guide
Aaniin is a greeting — it literally means something like "how is it?" or "what is the way of it?" It's used the way you'd say "hello" or "good morning." Elder Rose Swifthawk uses it at the opening of the Water Song recording. Want to try saying it back?
